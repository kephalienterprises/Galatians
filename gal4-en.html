Study Notes on Galatians

Assorted excerpts from different sources

Galatians 4

Galatians 4:1

λέγω δέ — Bruce translates this as “Let me put it this way,” or equivalently “I mean.” It is a transitional formula by which Paul introduces a clarifying illustration. [Bruce, Galatians]

The law has been compared, in the preceding argument, to a prison warden (3:23) and a slave attendant (3:24). Now its role is compared to that of the guardians and trustees appointed to take charge of a minor and his property. [Bruce, Galatians]

The picture Paul draws is of a boy in a home of wealth and standing who is legally the heir — and so the young master, lord, or owner of the family estate — but who is still a minor and so lives under rules very much like a slave. The picture is that of a family, and so is a pleasant one overall; certainly not a bad situation per se, but one judged to be inferior in comparison to adulthood. [Longenecker, Galatians]

Paul’s statement that there is no difference between a young boy in the family and a slave is, of course, hyperbole for the sake of the illustration. What he means is that they are alike in that they both live under rules and regulations. Looking back from the perspective of maturity, however, every adult acknowledges that in some ways minority status and slavery have much in common — whether the family experience was happy and beneficial or not. [Longenecker, Galatians]

Galatians 4:2

In Roman law, the heir, until he came of age at fourteen, was under the control of a tutor nominated by his father in his will; then, until he reached the age of twenty-five, he was under a curator appointed by the praetor Urbanus. [Bruce, Galatians; cf. Justinian, Institutiones 1.22–23]

The guardians and stewards of verse 2 are meant to be synonymous with the παιδαγωγός as supervisory guardian. This is particularly clear for ἐπίτροπος, which was a frequent term in Greek and became a loanword in Hebrew for the guardian of a minor. οἰκονόμος likewise appears frequently in Greek and as a loanword in Hebrew for one who acts as a steward or administrator for another. In this context, οἰκονόμος should be regarded as roughly synonymous with ἐπίτροπος. [Longenecker, Galatians]

Galatians 4:3

The first person plural “we” in verse 3, as in verse 5, should be understood as referring primarily to Jewish believers. [Longenecker, Galatians]

The man of NT days would take τὰ στοιχεῖα τοῦ κόσμου to refer to the “basic materials” of which everything in the cosmos, including man, is composed [Delling]. Paul, however, seems to have given this understanding a new twist. Building on the view of τὰ στοιχεῖα as “first principles” or “elementary teachings,” he asserts here in 4:3 that the Mosaic law comprised in the Jewish experience those “basic principles” given by God in preparation for the coming of Christ. [Longenecker, Galatians]

While τὰ στοιχεῖα τοῦ κόσμου would have been taken by Greek writers cosmologically — as the elements of the natural world — Paul takes κόσμος in an ethical sense to mean “worldly,” with its synonym being “fleshly” as opposed to “spiritual” (cf. Gal 5:13–26). Thus Jews under the law were under the basic principles of the world, with τοῦ κόσμος used here in much the same way as it appears later in Heb 9:1, where the tabernacle is called τὰ ἅγιον κοσμικόν (“a worldly sanctuary”). There is no doubt that the Mosaic law was given by God (as was the tabernacle for the author of Hebrews), but God has moved forward in the explication of his redemptive activity, and now to return to life “under the law” is to return to living under the basic principles of the world. [Longenecker, Galatians]

Here in 4:3 it was the Mosaic law in its condemnatory and supervisory functions that comprised the Jews’ “basic principles” of religion. Later in verse 9, when talking about the Gentile experience, it was paganism with its veneration of nature and cultic rituals that made up the Gentiles’ “basic principles” of religion. [Longenecker, Galatians]
